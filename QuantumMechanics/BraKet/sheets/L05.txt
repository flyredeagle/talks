# **Lecture 5 — Completeness, Change of Basis, and Unification of Representations**

### Dirac (abstract) ↔ Matrix mechanics ↔ Wavefunction (continuous basis)

***

## 90‑minute pacing

*   **0–15:** Why representations exist; “state is abstract, coordinates are basis-dependent”
*   **15–35:** Completeness and inserting identity (discrete); coordinate vectors
*   **35–55:** Change of basis as a unitary transformation; matrices and similarity transforms
*   **55–75:** Continuous basis: $$\int |x\rangle\langle x|dx=\mathbb I$$; wavefunction as projection
*   **75–90:** Operator kernels $$A(x,x')=\langle x|\hat A|x'\rangle$$; expectation values in $$x$$-rep; synthesis

3.  Perform unitary basis changes and compute transformed components

```latex
\section{Motivation: same state, different representations}
A quantum state is the abstract object $|\psi\rangle$. A \emph{representation} is obtained by choosing a basis and
computing components. Different bases correspond to different measurement contexts.

\begin{warnbox}
Do not confuse the state with its coordinate list. The list changes when you change basis, even though the state
does not.
\end{warnbox}

\section{Discrete basis: completeness and inserting identity}
Let $\{|n\rangle\}$ be an orthonormal basis:
\[
\langle m|n\rangle=\delta_{mn},\qquad \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|=\mathbb I.
\]
Insert the identity to expand any state:
\[
|\psi\rangle = \mathbb I|\psi\rangle = \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle.
\]
Define components (amplitudes in this basis):
\[
c_n=\langle n|\psi\rangle,\qquad |\psi\rangle=\sum_n c_n|n\rangle.
\]

\subsection{Inner product in components}
Insert identity twice:
\[
\langle\phi|\psi\rangle
=\sum_n \langle\phi|n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle
=\sum_n \phi_n^*\,\psi_n.
\]
This is the coordinate formula for inner products.

\section{Change of basis: unitary transformations}
Let $\{|n\rangle\}$ and $\{|\alpha\rangle\}$ be orthonormal bases. Define the overlap matrix
\[
U_{\alpha n}=\langle \alpha|n\rangle.
\]
Then
\[
\psi_\alpha=\langle \alpha|\psi\rangle
=\sum_n \langle \alpha|n\rangle \langle n|\psi\rangle
=\sum_n U_{\alpha n}\,c_n.
\]
So, in vector form:
\[
\mathbf c^{(\alpha)} = U\,\mathbf c^{(n)}.
\]
Because both bases are orthonormal, $U$ is unitary.

\section{Operators: abstract $\leftrightarrow$ matrix}
Define matrix elements in basis $\{|n\rangle\}$:
\[
A_{mn}=\langle m|\hat A|n\rangle.
\]
Then for coefficient vectors $\mathbf c$,
\[
(\hat A|\psi\rangle)_m = \sum_n A_{mn}c_n,
\]
i.e. $\mathbf c' = A\mathbf c$.

\subsection{Similarity under basis change}
If $U$ changes basis, the matrix of the same abstract operator becomes
\[
A' = UAU^\dagger
\quad\text{(convention: coefficients transform as } \mathbf c'=U\mathbf c\text{)}.
\]

\section{Continuous basis: wavefunctions as projections}
Introduce position kets $|x\rangle$ (formal for now) satisfying completeness:
\[
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx\,|x\rangle\langle x|=\mathbb I,
\qquad \psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle.
\]
Then expansion becomes
\[
|\psi\rangle=\int dx\,|x\rangle\psi(x).
\]

\subsection{Inner product in wavefunctions}
Insert the continuous identity:
\[
\langle\phi|\psi\rangle
=\int dx\,\langle\phi|x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle
=\int dx\,\phi^*(x)\psi(x).
\]

\section{Operators in the $x$-representation: kernels}
Define the kernel
\[
A(x,x')=\langle x|\hat A|x'\rangle.
\]
Then
\[
(\hat A\psi)(x)=\langle x|\hat A|\psi\rangle
=\int dx'\,\langle x|\hat A|x'\rangle\langle x'|\psi\rangle
=\int dx'\,A(x,x')\psi(x').
\]
Expectation values become
\[
\langle \hat A\rangle
=\langle\psi|\hat A|\psi\rangle
=\int dx\,dx'\,\psi^*(x)\,A(x,x')\,\psi(x').
\]

\begin{mapbox}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Abstract (Dirac)} & \textbf{Matrix basis }|n\rangle & \textbf{Wavefunction basis }|x\rangle\\ \midrule
$|\psi\rangle$ & $c_n=\langle n|\psi\rangle$ & $\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle$\\
$\mathbb I$ & $\sum_n|n\rangle\langle n|$ & $\int dx\,|x\rangle\langle x|$\\
$\hat A$ & $A_{mn}=\langle m|\hat A|n\rangle$ & $A(x,x')=\langle x|\hat A|x'\rangle$\\
$\hat A|\psi\rangle$ & $c'_m=\sum_n A_{mn}c_n$ & $(A\psi)(x)=\int dx'\,A(x,x')\psi(x')$\\
$\langle\phi|\psi\rangle$ & $\sum_n \phi_n^*\psi_n$ & $\int dx\,\phi^*(x)\psi(x)$\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{mapbox}
```

## **S1 — Discrete expansion via completeness**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L05-S1 Discrete expansion — Problem}
Let $\{|n\rangle\}$ be an ONB with $\sum_n|n\rangle\langle n|=I$. Show $|\psi\rangle=\sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle$ and identify $c_n$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Insert $I$ in front of $|\psi\rangle$.
\item Define $c_n=\langle n|\psi\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Interpret $c_n$ as amplitude for outcome $n$ in that measurement basis.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
$|\psi\rangle=I|\psi\rangle=\left(\sum_n|n\rangle\langle n|\right)|\psi\rangle=\sum_n|n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle$.
Define $c_n=\langle n|\psi\rangle$, giving $|\psi\rangle=\sum_n c_n|n\rangle$.
\end{document}
```

## **S2 — Inner product in components**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L05-S2 Inner product (discrete) — Problem}
Let $|\psi\rangle=\sum_n\psi_n|n\rangle$ and $|\phi\rangle=\sum_n\phi_n|n\rangle$. Prove $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=\sum_n \phi_n^*\psi_n$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Write $\langle\phi|=\sum_n \phi_n^*\langle n|$.
\item Multiply out and use $\langle n|m\rangle=\delta_{nm}$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Alternatively insert identity: $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=\sum_n\langle\phi|n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
$\langle\phi|=\sum_n\phi_n^*\langle n|$. Then
$\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=\sum_{n,m}\phi_n^*\psi_m\langle n|m\rangle=\sum_n\phi_n^*\psi_n$.
\end{document}
```

## **S3 — Change of basis coefficients**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L05-S3 Basis change coefficients — Problem}
Let $\{|n\rangle\}$ and $\{|\alpha\rangle\}$ be ONBs. Define $U_{\alpha n}=\langle\alpha|n\rangle$ and $c_n=\langle n|\psi\rangle$.
Show $\psi_\alpha=\langle\alpha|\psi\rangle=\sum_n U_{\alpha n}c_n$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Insert $I=\sum_n|n\rangle\langle n|$ between $\langle\alpha|$ and $|\psi\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Interpret $U$ as change-of-basis matrix between measurement contexts.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
$\psi_\alpha=\langle\alpha|\psi\rangle=\sum_n\langle\alpha|n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle=\sum_n U_{\alpha n}c_n$.
\end{document}
```

## **S4 — Unitary preserves normalization**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L05-S4 Unitary preserves norm — Problem}
Let $c' = Uc$ with $U^\dagger U=I$. Show $\sum_\alpha |c'_\alpha|^2=\sum_n |c_n|^2$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Use vector notation: $\|c\|^2=c^\dagger c$.
\item Compute $(Uc)^\dagger(Uc)$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Interpret as basis-independence of $\langle\psi|\psi\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
$\|c'\|^2=(Uc)^\dagger(Uc)=c^\dagger U^\dagger U c=c^\dagger c=\|c\|^2$.
\end{document}
```

## **S5 — Matrix multiplication from completeness**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L05-S5 Matrix product rule — Problem}
Let $A_{mn}=\langle m|\hat A|n\rangle$ and $B_{mn}=\langle m|\hat B|n\rangle$ in ONB $\{|n\rangle\}$.
Show $(AB)_{mn}=\sum_k A_{mk}B_{kn}$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Write $(AB)_{mn}=\langle m|\hat A\hat B|n\rangle$.
\item Insert $I=\sum_k |k\rangle\langle k|$ between $\hat A$ and $\hat B$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Recognize the derivation of standard matrix multiplication from Dirac notation.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
$(AB)_{mn}=\langle m|\hat A\hat B|n\rangle=\sum_k \langle m|\hat A|k\rangle\langle k|\hat B|n\rangle=\sum_k A_{mk}B_{kn}$.
\end{document}
```

## **S6 — Expectation value: Dirac ↔ matrix**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L05-S6 Expectation value equivalence — Problem}
Let $|\psi\rangle=\sum_n c_n|n\rangle$ and $A_{mn}=\langle m|\hat A|n\rangle$. Show
$\langle\psi|\hat A|\psi\rangle=\sum_{mn} c_m^*A_{mn}c_n = c^\dagger A c$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Expand bras/kets and use bilinearity.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Insert identities on both sides of $\hat A$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
$\langle\psi|=\sum_m c_m^*\langle m|$, so
$\langle\psi|\hat A|\psi\rangle=\sum_{mn} c_m^*\langle m|\hat A|n\rangle c_n=\sum_{mn}c_m^*A_{mn}c_n=c^\dagger A c$.
\end{document}
```

## **S7 — Wavefunction as projection**


## **S8 — Inner product as integral**


## **C2 — Similarity transform of operator matrices**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L05-C2 Operator transforms under basis change — Problem}
Assume coefficient vectors transform as $c'=Uc$ when switching from basis $|n\rangle$ to $|\alpha\rangle$.
Show that the matrix representing the same operator satisfies $A' = UAU^\dagger$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Require that applying the operator then changing basis equals changing basis then applying the new matrix:
$c'_{\text{out}} = U(Ac)$ and also $c'_{\text{out}} = A'(Uc)$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Use equality for all $c$ to deduce $UA = A'U$ and hence $A'=UAU^\dagger$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
We demand representation consistency:
\[
c'_{\text{out}} = U(Ac) \quad\text{and}\quad c'_{\text{out}} = A'(Uc).
\]
Thus for all $c$, $UA c = A'U c$, so $UA = A'U$.
Multiply on the right by $U^\dagger$: $UAU^\dagger = A'(UU^\dagger)=A'$.
\end{document}
```

```latex
\section{Projective measurements (PVM) and L\"uders rule}
A projective measurement of $A$ is described by orthogonal projectors $\{P_a\}$ with
$P_aP_b=\delta_{ab}P_a$ and $\sum_a P_a=I$ (degeneracy allowed: $P_a$ projects onto the eigenspace).

Probability of outcome $a$ in pure state $|\psi\rangle$:
\[
p(a)=\langle\psi|P_a|\psi\rangle.
\]
L\"uders state update (pure state):
\[
|\psi\rangle \mapsto \frac{P_a|\psi\rangle}{\sqrt{\langle\psi|P_a|\psi\rangle}}.
\]
Density-matrix form:
\[
\rho \mapsto \rho_a'=\frac{P_a\rho P_a}{\Tr(P_a\rho)}.
\]
If the outcome is ignored, the post-measurement state is
\[
\rho'=\sum_a P_a\rho P_a.
\]

\begin{warnbox}
For degenerate outcomes, collapsing to a specific eigenvector adds information not contained in the measurement.
L\"uders projects onto the eigenspace and preserves exactly what was learned.
\end{warnbox}

\section{Compatibility and commutators}
Two sharp (projective) observables $A$ and $B$ are compatible if they can be measured jointly without mutual
disturbance in the idealized sense. A sufficient condition (and in finite dimension essentially equivalent) is
\[
[\hat A,\hat B]=0,
\]
which implies they share a common eigenbasis (simultaneous diagonalization).

\section{Variance and uncertainty}
For an observable $\hat A$ in state $|\psi\rangle$ define
\[
\langle A\rangle=\langle\psi|\hat A|\psi\rangle,\qquad (\Delta A)^2=\langle (\hat A-\langle A\rangle)^2\rangle.
\]
Let $\Delta\hat A=\hat A-\langle A\rangle I$.

\subsection{Robertson uncertainty relation}
Consider the nonnegative norm
\[
\|(\Delta\hat A + i\lambda \Delta\hat B)|\psi\rangle\|^2\ge 0
\quad \text{for all real }\lambda.
\]
Expanding yields a quadratic in $\lambda$ whose discriminant must be nonpositive, giving
\[
\Delta A\,\Delta B \ge \frac12\,|\langle[\hat A,\hat B]\rangle|.
\]

\subsection{Schr\"odinger uncertainty relation}
A stronger inequality includes the covariance:
\[
(\Delta A)^2(\Delta B)^2 \ge \left(\frac12|\langle[\hat A,\hat B]\rangle|\right)^2
+ \left(\frac12\langle\{\Delta\hat A,\Delta\hat B\}\rangle\right)^2,
\]
where $\{X,Y\}=XY+YX$.

\section{POVM preview: generalized measurements}
Projective measurements are not the most general. A POVM is a set of positive operators (effects)
\[
E_i\ge 0,\qquad \sum_i E_i=I.
\]
Probabilities:
\[
p(i)=\langle\psi|E_i|\psi\rangle \quad (\text{or } p(i)=\Tr(\rho E_i)).
\]
Projective measurement is the special case $E_i=P_i$ with orthogonal projectors.

\subsection{Kraus operators and state update (instrument)}
Often a POVM arises from measurement operators $M_i$ (Kraus operators) such that
\[
E_i=M_i^\dagger M_i,\qquad \sum_i M_i^\dagger M_i=I.
\]
Then
\[
p(i)=\Tr(M_i\rho M_i^\dagger),
\qquad
\rho \mapsto \rho_i'=\frac{M_i\rho M_i^\dagger}{p(i)}.
\]
Ignoring outcomes gives a quantum channel:
\[
\rho'=\sum_i M_i\rho M_i^\dagger.
\]

\begin{mapbox}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Sharp (PVM)} & \textbf{General (POVM)} & \textbf{Update rule}\\ \midrule
$P_a$ projectors, $\sum_a P_a=I$ & $E_i\ge 0$, $\sum_i E_i=I$ & $|\psi\rangle\to P_a|\psi\rangle/\sqrt{p(a)}$\\
prob $p(a)=\langle\psi|P_a|\psi\rangle$ & prob $p(i)=\langle\psi|E_i|\psi\rangle$ & $\rho\to M_i\rho M_i^\dagger/p(i)$\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{mapbox}
```

## **S1 — Variance in 2×2**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}
\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}
\geometry{margin=1in}
\begin{document}

\section*{L07-S1 Variance — Problem}
Let $A=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$ and $|\psi\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.
Compute $\langle A\rangle$ and $(\Delta A)^2$.

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Use $\langle A\rangle=\psi^\dagger A\psi$.
\item Use $(\Delta A)^2=\langle A^2\rangle-\langle A\rangle^2$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Note $A^2=I$ for this matrix.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
$\psi=(1/\sqrt2,1/\sqrt2)^T$.
\[
\langle A\rangle=\psi^\dagger A\psi=(1/\sqrt2,1/\sqrt2)\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1/\sqrt2\\1/\sqrt2\end{pmatrix}=0.
\]
Since $A^2=I$, $\langle A^2\rangle=\psi^\dagger I\psi=1$.
Thus $(\Delta A)^2=1-0^2=1$.
\end{document}
```

## **S2 — Commutator**

```latex

\section*{L07-S2 Commutator — Problem}
Let $A=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$ and $B=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$.
Compute $[A,B]=AB-BA$.

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Multiply matrices explicitly.

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Recognize these as $\sigma_z$ and $\sigma_x$.

\[
AB=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{pmatrix},\quad
BA=\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Thus
\[
[A,B]=AB-BA=\begin{pmatrix}0&2\\-2&0\end{pmatrix}=2\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{pmatrix}.
\]
```

## **S3 — Compatibility example**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}
\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}
\geometry{margin=1in}
\begin{document}

\section*{L07-S3 Compatibility — Problem}
Let $A=\mathrm{diag}(1,2)$ and $B=\mathrm{diag}(3,4)$. Show $[A,B]=0$ and explain why they are compatible (finite-dim case).

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Diagonal matrices commute.
\item Recall commuting Hermitian matrices share an eigenbasis.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Explain that both are diagonal in the same basis, so can be measured together in that basis.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
$AB=\mathrm{diag}(3,8)=BA$, hence $[A,B]=0$.
In finite dimension, commuting Hermitian operators are simultaneously diagonalizable, so there exists a basis of joint eigenvectors; measuring in that basis yields both values without ambiguity (compatibility in the ideal projective sense).
\end{document}
```

## **S4 — Qubit variances**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}
\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}
\geometry{margin=1in}
\begin{document}

\section*{L07-S4 Qubit variances — Problem}
Let $|\psi\rangle=\cos(\theta/2)|0\rangle+\sin(\theta/2)|1\rangle$ (real amplitudes).
Compute $\langle\sigma_z\rangle$ and $\Delta\sigma_z$.

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Use $\langle\sigma_z\rangle=|\alpha|^2-|\beta|^2$.
\item Use $\sigma_z^2=I$ so $\Delta^2=1-\langle\sigma_z\rangle^2$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Express $\langle\sigma_z\rangle=\cos\theta$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
Here $\alpha=\cos(\theta/2)$, $\beta=\sin(\theta/2)$.
\[
\langle\sigma_z\rangle=\cos^2(\theta/2)-\sin^2(\theta/2)=\cos\theta.
\]
Since $\sigma_z^2=I$, $\langle\sigma_z^2\rangle=1$, hence
\[
(\Delta\sigma_z)^2=1-\cos^2\theta=\sin^2\theta,\quad \Delta\sigma_z=|\sin\theta|.
\]
\end{document}
```

## **S5 — Robertson bound for $$\sigma_x,\sigma_z$$**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}
\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}
\geometry{margin=1in}
\begin{document}

\section*{L07-S5 Robertson bound — Problem}
For a normalized state $|\psi\rangle$ in $\mathbb C^2$, let $A=\sigma_x$, $B=\sigma_z$.
Show the Robertson bound reads $\Delta\sigma_x\,\Delta\sigma_z \ge |\langle\sigma_y\rangle|$.

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Use $[\sigma_x,\sigma_z]=2i\sigma_y$.
\item Insert into $\Delta A\Delta B \ge \frac12|\langle[A,B]\rangle|$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Check factors: $\frac12|2i\langle\sigma_y\rangle|=|\langle\sigma_y\rangle|$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
Since $[\sigma_x,\sigma_z]=2i\sigma_y$, Robertson gives
\[
\Delta\sigma_x\,\Delta\sigma_z \ge \frac12 | \langle 2i\sigma_y\rangle|
= |\langle\sigma_y\rangle|.
\]
\end{document}
```

## **S6 — PVM probability + Lüders update**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}
\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}
\geometry{margin=1in}
\begin{document}

\section*{L07-S6 L\"uders update (nondegenerate) — Problem}
Let $P_0=|0\rangle\langle0|$ and $|\psi\rangle=\alpha|0\rangle+\beta|1\rangle$.
Compute $p(0)$ and the post-measurement state given outcome 0.

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item $p(0)=\langle\psi|P_0|\psi\rangle$.
\item Updated state: $P_0|\psi\rangle/\sqrt{p(0)}$ if $p(0)>0$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Note $P_0|\psi\rangle=\alpha|0\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
\[
p(0)=\langle\psi|0\rangle\langle0|\psi\rangle=|\alpha|^2.
\]
If $|\alpha|^2>0$, then
\[
|\psi'\rangle=\frac{\alpha|0\rangle}{|\alpha|}=e^{i\arg\alpha}|0\rangle \sim |0\rangle.
\]
Up to global phase, the state becomes $|0\rangle$.
```

## **C1 — Robertson uncertainty derivation**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}
\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}
\geometry{margin=1in}
\begin{document}

\section*{L07-C1 Robertson inequality — Problem}
Derive $\Delta A\,\Delta B \ge \frac12|\langle[A,B]\rangle|$.

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Let $\Delta\hat A=\hat A-\langle A\rangle$, $\Delta\hat B=\hat B-\langle B\rangle$.
\item Consider $\|(\Delta\hat A+i\lambda\Delta\hat B)|\psi\rangle\|^2\ge0$ for real $\lambda$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Expand as a quadratic in $\lambda$ and require discriminant $\le0$.
\item Use $\langle\Delta A\Delta B\rangle-\langle\Delta B\Delta A\rangle=\langle[A,B]\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
Let $|\chi\rangle=(\Delta\hat A+i\lambda\Delta\hat B)|\psi\rangle$. Then $0\le\langle\chi|\chi\rangle$ gives:
\[
0\le (\Delta A)^2 + \lambda^2(\Delta B)^2 + i\lambda\langle[\Delta\hat A,\Delta\hat B]\rangle.
\]
But $[\Delta\hat A,\Delta\hat B]=[\hat A,\hat B]$. Write $\langle[\hat A,\hat B]\rangle = iC$ with $C\in\mathbb R$ for Hermitian $A,B$.
Then inequality is
\[
0\le (\Delta A)^2 + \lambda^2(\Delta B)^2 - \lambda C.
\]
As a quadratic in $\lambda$, discriminant must satisfy $C^2 \le 4(\Delta A)^2(\Delta B)^2$, hence
\[
\Delta A\,\Delta B \ge \frac{|C|}{2}=\frac12|\langle[\hat A,\hat B]\rangle|.
\]
\end{document}
```

## **C2 — Schrödinger uncertainty derivation**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}
\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}
\geometry{margin=1in}
\begin{document}

\section*{L07-C2 Schr\"odinger inequality — Problem}
Derive
\[
(\Delta A)^2(\Delta B)^2 \ge \left(\frac12|\langle[A,B]\rangle|\right)^2 +
\left(\frac12\langle\{\Delta A,\Delta B\}\rangle\right)^2.
\]

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Use Cauchy--Schwarz on vectors $|\alpha\rangle=\Delta\hat A|\psi\rangle$ and $|\beta\rangle=\Delta\hat B|\psi\rangle$.
\item Write $\langle\alpha|\beta\rangle=\langle\Delta A\,\Delta B\rangle$ and split into real/imag parts.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Use $\langle\Delta A\,\Delta B\rangle = \frac12\langle\{\Delta A,\Delta B\}\rangle + \frac12\langle[\Delta A,\Delta B]\rangle$.
\item Then $|\langle\alpha|\beta\rangle|^2 = (\Re)^2 + (\Im)^2$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
Cauchy--Schwarz gives $|\langle\alpha|\beta\rangle|^2 \le \langle\alpha|\alpha\rangle\langle\beta|\beta\rangle=(\Delta A)^2(\Delta B)^2$.
Now
\[
\langle\alpha|\beta\rangle=\langle\psi|\Delta\hat A\,\Delta\hat B|\psi\rangle
=\frac12\langle\{\Delta\hat A,\Delta\hat B\}\rangle + \frac12\langle[\Delta\hat A,\Delta\hat B]\rangle.
\]
The first term is real; the second is purely imaginary for Hermitian $A,B$.
Thus
\[
|\langle\alpha|\beta\rangle|^2 = \left(\frac12\langle\{\Delta A,\Delta B\}\rangle\right)^2
+ \left(\frac12|\langle[A,B]\rangle|\right)^2.
\]
Combine with C--S bound to obtain Schr\"odinger inequality.
\end{document}
```

## **C3 — Lüders update preserves normalization**

```latex

\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}
\geometry{margin=1in}

\section*{L07-C3 L\"uders normalization — Problem}
Show L\"uders update preserves normalization for (i) pure state $|\psi\rangle$ and (ii) density matrix $\rho$.

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item For pure: compute $\langle\psi'|\psi'\rangle$ and use $P_a^2=P_a$.
\item For density: show $\Tr(\rho'_a)=1$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Use $P_a^\dagger=P_a$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
(i) $|\psi'\rangle=P_a|\psi\rangle/\sqrt{\langle\psi|P_a|\psi\rangle}$:
\[
\langle\psi'|\psi'\rangle=\frac{\langle\psi|P_aP_a|\psi\rangle}{\langle\psi|P_a|\psi\rangle}
=\frac{\langle\psi|P_a|\psi\rangle}{\langle\psi|P_a|\psi\rangle}=1.
\]
(ii) $\rho'_a=P_a\rho P_a/\Tr(P_a\rho)$:
\[
\Tr(\rho'_a)=\frac{\Tr(P_a\rho P_a)}{\Tr(P_a\rho)}=\frac{\Tr(P_a^2\rho)}{\Tr(P_a\rho)}=1.
\]
```

## **C4 — Kraus completeness ⇒ probabilities sum to 1 and channel**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}
\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}
\geometry{margin=1in}
\begin{document}

\section*{L07-C4 Kraus completeness — Problem}
Let $\{M_i\}$ satisfy $\sum_i M_i^\dagger M_i=I$. Show:
(i) $p(i)=\Tr(M_i\rho M_i^\dagger)$ sums to 1.
(ii) $\rho'=\sum_i M_i\rho M_i^\dagger$ is trace-preserving.

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Use cyclicity of trace: $\Tr(M_i\rho M_i^\dagger)=\Tr(\rho M_i^\dagger M_i)$.
\item Sum over $i$ and use completeness.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Interpret $\rho'$ as the state when the measurement outcome is ignored.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution}
(i)
\[
\sum_i p(i)=\sum_i \Tr(M_i\rho M_i^\dagger)=\sum_i \Tr(\rho M_i^\dagger M_i)
=\Tr\!\left(\rho\sum_i M_i^\dagger M_i\right)=\Tr(\rho I)=\Tr(\rho)=1.
\]
(ii)
\[
\Tr(\rho')=\Tr\left(\sum_i M_i\rho M_i^\dagger\right)=\sum_i \Tr(\rho M_i^\dagger M_i)=\Tr(\rho)=1.
\]
\end{document}
```

```latex
\section*{L07-C5 Naimark dilation (preview) — Problem}
Provide a structured proof sketch (1--2 pages) of the statement:
``Any POVM on $\mathcal H$ can be realized as a projective measurement on a larger Hilbert space.''

\newpage
\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Think: system + ancilla, unitary interaction, then projective measurement on ancilla.
\item Use Kraus operators as the bridge.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Outline: Stinespring dilation for channels and then specialize to measurement.
\item Show effects arise from $E_i=V^\dagger (I\otimes |i\rangle\langle i|)V$ for an isometry $V$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage
\section*{Detailed solution (model sketch)}
A good sketch states:
\begin{itemize}
\item Any POVM can be implemented by measurement operators $M_i$ with $\sum_i M_i^\dagger M_i=I$.
\item Define an ancilla space with basis $|i\rangle$ and an isometry $V:\mathcal H\to\mathcal H\otimes\mathcal K$ by
$V|\psi\rangle=\sum_i (M_i|\psi\rangle)\otimes |i\rangle$.
\item Then $V^\dagger V=I$ due to Kraus completeness.
\item A projective measurement $\{I\otimes |i\rangle\langle i|\}$ on the ancilla yields probability
$\langle\psi|V^\dagger(I\otimes |i\rangle\langle i|)V|\psi\rangle=\langle\psi|E_i|\psi\rangle$ where $E_i=M_i^\dagger M_i$.
\end{itemize}
```

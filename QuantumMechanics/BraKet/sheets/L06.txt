# **Lecture 6 — Continuous Bases: $$|x\rangle$$, $$|p\rangle$$, Delta Functions, Fourier Transform**

### Dirac (abstract) ↔ Matrix/Kernel ↔ Wavefunction representations (fully unified)

## 90‑minute pacing

*   **0–15:** Why continuous bases are “generalized”; measurement with continuous outcomes
*   **15–35:** Position basis: $$\langle x|x'\rangle=\delta(x-x')$$, $$\int |x\rangle\langle x|dx=\mathbb I$$

5.  Compute expectation values in integral form and identify where delta/distributions are formal

```latex
\section{Continuous outcomes and generalized bases}
In many measurements (e.g. position), outcomes are continuous. We model this by a continuum of kets $|x\rangle$.
These kets are \emph{generalized} eigenstates: they are not normalizable in the usual sense, and their overlaps
involve the Dirac delta distribution.

\begin{warnbox}
$|x\rangle$ is not a normal element of $\mathcal H$ (it is delta-normalized). Treat it as a formal tool whose
consistent use is justified later (rigged Hilbert spaces).
\end{warnbox}

\section{Position basis}
Postulate (formal):
\[
\langle x|x'\rangle=\delta(x-x'),\qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx\,|x\rangle\langle x| = I.
\]
Define the wavefunction:
\[
\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle.
\]
Then expansion follows by inserting identity:
\[
|\psi\rangle = \int dx\,|x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle = \int dx\,|x\rangle\,\psi(x).
\]

\subsection{Inner product}
\[
\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=\int dx\,\langle\phi|x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle
=\int dx\,\phi^*(x)\psi(x).
\]

\section{Momentum basis and Fourier transform}
Similarly define momentum kets $|p\rangle$ with
\[
\langle p|p'\rangle=\delta(p-p'),\qquad \int dp\,|p\rangle\langle p|=I,\qquad \phi(p)=\langle p|\psi\rangle.
\]
Choose the standard convention:
\[
\langle x|p\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{ipx/\hbar}.
\]
Insert the momentum identity to relate representations:
\[
\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle=\int dp\,\langle x|p\rangle\langle p|\psi\rangle
=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dp\,e^{ipx/\hbar}\phi(p).
\]
Similarly,
\[
\phi(p)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dx\,e^{-ipx/\hbar}\psi(x).
\]
Thus the change of basis between $x$ and $p$ is a Fourier transform.

\section{Operators in the $x$-representation}
\subsection{Position operator}
Because $\hat x|x\rangle = x|x\rangle$, we get
\[
(\hat x\psi)(x)=\langle x|\hat x|\psi\rangle = x\langle x|\psi\rangle = x\psi(x).
\]

\subsection{Momentum operator}
Because $\hat p|p\rangle = p|p\rangle$, we compute
\[
\langle x|\hat p|\psi\rangle = \int dp\,\langle x|p\rangle\,p\,\phi(p).
\]
Using $\langle x|p\rangle\propto e^{ipx/\hbar}$, note that $p e^{ipx/\hbar} = -i\hbar\,\partial_x e^{ipx/\hbar}$.
Move $-i\hbar\partial_x$ outside the integral (formal):
\[
(\hat p\psi)(x) = -i\hbar\,\frac{d}{dx}\psi(x).
\]

\section{Kernels and expectation values}
Define the kernel of an operator:
\[
A(x,x')=\langle x|\hat A|x'\rangle.
\]
Then
\[
(\hat A\psi)(x)=\int dx'\,A(x,x')\psi(x').
\]
Expectation value:
\[
\langle\hat A\rangle=\langle\psi|\hat A|\psi\rangle = \int dx\,dx'\,\psi^*(x)A(x,x')\psi(x').
\]
For $\hat x$, the kernel is $x\delta(x-x')$; for $\hat p$, it is $-i\hbar\,\partial_x\delta(x-x')$ (formal).

\begin{mapbox}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Dirac} & \textbf{$x$-rep} & \textbf{$p$-rep}\\ \midrule
$\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle$ & wavefunction & $\phi(p)=\langle p|\psi\rangle$\\
$I=\int dx\,|x\rangle\langle x|$ & completeness & $I=\int dp\,|p\rangle\langle p|$\\
$\langle x|p\rangle$ & kernel & Fourier phase\\
$\hat x$ & $(x\psi)(x)$ & $i\hbar\,\partial_p\phi(p)$ (preview)\\
$\hat p$ & $-i\hbar\partial_x\psi(x)$ & $(p\phi)(p)$\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{mapbox}
```

## **S1 — Expand ket in position basis**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-S1 Expansion in $x$-basis — Problem}
Assume $\int dx\,|x\rangle\langle x|=I$. Define $\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle$ and show
$|\psi\rangle=\int dx\,|x\rangle\psi(x)$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Insert the identity in front of $|\psi\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Interpret $\psi(x)$ as the coordinate of the abstract ket in the $x$ basis.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
$|\psi\rangle=I|\psi\rangle=\int dx\,|x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle=\int dx\,|x\rangle\psi(x)$.
\end{document}
```

## **S2 — Inner product as an integral**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-S2 Inner product — Problem}
Assume $\int dx\,|x\rangle\langle x|=I$. Show $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=\int dx\,\phi^*(x)\psi(x)$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Insert the identity between $\langle\phi|$ and $|\psi\rangle$.
\item Use $\langle\phi|x\rangle=\phi^*(x)$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Compare to discrete formula $\sum_n \phi_n^*\psi_n$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
$\langle\phi|\psi\rangle=\int dx\,\langle\phi|x\rangle\langle x|\psi\rangle=\int dx\,\phi^*(x)\psi(x)$.
\end{document}
```

## **S3 — Delta “sifting”**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-S3 Sifting — Problem}
Assuming the sifting property of $\delta$, evaluate $\int dx\,f(x)\delta(x-a)$ and state the result.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Recall $\delta(x-a)$ ``picks out'' the value at $x=a$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Mention that $\delta$ is a distribution, not an ordinary function.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
By definition of the Dirac delta distribution,
\[
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\delta(x-a)\,dx=f(a).
\]
\end{document}
```

## **S4 — Delta with shifted argument**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
```

## **S5 — Fourier relation from $$\langle x|p\rangle$$**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-S5 Fourier transform — Problem}
Assume $\langle x|p\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{ipx/\hbar}$ and $I=\int dp\,|p\rangle\langle p|$.
Show $\psi(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dp\,e^{ipx/\hbar}\phi(p)$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Insert $I$ in $\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Identify $\phi(p)=\langle p|\psi\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
\[
\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle=\int dp\,\langle x|p\rangle\langle p|\psi\rangle
=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dp\,e^{ipx/\hbar}\phi(p).
\]
\end{document}
```

## **S6 — Normalization consistency (stated)**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-S6 Norm consistency — Problem}
Assume Parseval/Plancherel holds for the chosen normalization. Show that if $\int dx|\psi(x)|^2=1$ then $\int dp|\phi(p)|^2=1$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Use $\int dx|\psi(x)|^2=\int dp|\phi(p)|^2$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Note: the proof is asked in C1.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
By Parseval/Plancherel, $\int dx|\psi(x)|^2=\int dp|\phi(p)|^2$. If the left side equals 1, the right side equals 1.
\end{document}
```

## **S7 — $$\hat x$$ acts by multiplication**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-S7 Position operator — Problem}
Using $\hat x|x\rangle=x|x\rangle$, show $(\hat x\psi)(x)=x\psi(x)$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Start from $(\hat x\psi)(x)=\langle x|\hat x|\psi\rangle$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Insert $I=\int dx'|x'\rangle\langle x'|$ if needed.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
\[
(\hat x\psi)(x)=\langle x|\hat x|\psi\rangle=\langle x|x|\psi\rangle=x\langle x|\psi\rangle=x\psi(x).
\]
\end{document}
```

## **S8 — Derive $$\hat p=-i\hbar\partial_x$$**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb,physics}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-S8 Momentum operator — Problem}
Assume $\langle x|p\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}e^{ipx/\hbar}$ and $\phi(p)=\langle p|\psi\rangle$.
Show $(\hat p\psi)(x)=-i\hbar\,\partial_x\psi(x)$ (formal).

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Write $(\hat p\psi)(x)=\langle x|\hat p|\psi\rangle=\int dp\,\langle x|p\rangle\,p\,\phi(p)$.
\item Use $p e^{ipx/\hbar}=-i\hbar \partial_x e^{ipx/\hbar}$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Move $\partial_x$ outside the integral and recognize the remaining integral as $\psi(x)$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
\[
(\hat p\psi)(x)=\int dp\,\langle x|p\rangle\,p\,\phi(p)
=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dp\,p\,e^{ipx/\hbar}\phi(p).
\]
Since $p e^{ipx/\hbar}=-i\hbar\partial_x e^{ipx/\hbar}$,
\[
(\hat p\psi)(x)= -i\hbar\,\partial_x\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dp\,e^{ipx/\hbar}\phi(p)\right)
=-i\hbar\,\partial_x\psi(x).
\]
\end{document}
```

## **C1 — Parseval/Plancherel proof**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-C1 Parseval/Plancherel — Problem}
With $\psi(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dp\,e^{ipx/\hbar}\phi(p)$ and
$\phi(p)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dx\,e^{-ipx/\hbar}\psi(x)$, prove
\[
\int dx\,|\psi(x)|^2=\int dp\,|\phi(p)|^2.
\]

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Write $\int dx\,\psi^*(x)\psi(x)$ and substitute the Fourier forms.
\item Use $\int dx\,e^{i(p-p')x/\hbar}=2\pi\hbar\,\delta(p-p')$ (distribution identity).
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Keep track of constants carefully; they cancel with $1/(2\pi\hbar)$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
Start:
\[
\int dx\,|\psi(x)|^2=\int dx\,\psi^*(x)\psi(x).
\]
Insert Fourier forms:
\[
\psi(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dp\,e^{ipx/\hbar}\phi(p),\quad
\psi^*(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}}\int dp'\,e^{-ip'x/\hbar}\phi^*(p').
\]
Thus
\[
\int dx\,|\psi(x)|^2=\frac{1}{2\pi\hbar}\int dp\,dp'\,\phi^*(p')\phi(p)\int dx\,e^{i(p-p')x/\hbar}.
\]
Use $\int dx\,e^{i(p-p')x/\hbar}=2\pi\hbar\,\delta(p-p')$:
\[
\int dx\,|\psi(x)|^2=\int dp\,dp'\,\phi^*(p')\phi(p)\delta(p-p')=\int dp\,|\phi(p)|^2.
\]
\end{document}
```

## **C2 — Normalize $$\langle x|p\rangle$$**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-C2 Normalization of $\langle x|p\rangle$ — Problem}
Assume $\langle x|p\rangle=C\,e^{ipx/\hbar}$. Determine $C$ by requiring $\langle p|p'\rangle=\delta(p-p')$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Use $\langle p|p'\rangle=\int dx\,\langle p|x\rangle\langle x|p'\rangle$.
\item Note $\langle p|x\rangle=\langle x|p\rangle^*$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Use $\int dx\,e^{i(p'-p)x/\hbar}=2\pi\hbar\,\delta(p'-p)$.
\item Solve $|C|^2(2\pi\hbar)=1$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
Compute
\[
\langle p|p'\rangle=\int dx\,\langle p|x\rangle\langle x|p'\rangle
=\int dx\,C^*e^{-ipx/\hbar}\,C e^{ip'x/\hbar}
=|C|^2\int dx\,e^{i(p'-p)x/\hbar}.
\]
Using $\int dx\,e^{i(p'-p)x/\hbar}=2\pi\hbar\,\delta(p'-p)$ gives
\[
\langle p|p'\rangle = |C|^2(2\pi\hbar)\delta(p'-p).
\]
To match $\delta(p'-p)$, require $|C|^2(2\pi\hbar)=1$, hence
\[
C=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\hbar}} \quad \text{(choose positive real)}.
\]
\end{document}
```

## **C3 — Commutator $$[\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar$$**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-C3 Canonical commutator — Problem}
Using $(\hat x\psi)(x)=x\psi(x)$ and $(\hat p\psi)(x)=-i\hbar\partial_x\psi(x)$, show
\[
([\hat x,\hat p]\psi)(x)=i\hbar\,\psi(x)
\]
for sufficiently smooth $\psi$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Compute $(\hat x\hat p\psi)(x)$ and $(\hat p\hat x\psi)(x)$.
\item Subtract.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Use product rule: $\partial_x(x\psi)=\psi+x\partial_x\psi$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
Compute:
\[
(\hat x\hat p\psi)(x)=x(-i\hbar\partial_x\psi(x))=-i\hbar x\psi'(x).
\]
Next
\[
(\hat p\hat x\psi)(x)=-i\hbar\partial_x(x\psi(x))=-i\hbar(\psi(x)+x\psi'(x)).
\]
Subtract:
\[
([\hat x,\hat p]\psi)(x)=(\hat x\hat p-\hat p\hat x)\psi
=-i\hbar x\psi'(x)-\big[-i\hbar(\psi+x\psi')\big]=i\hbar\psi(x).
\]
\end{document}
```

## **C4 — Translation operator acts as shift**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry,amsmath,amssymb}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-C4 Translation operator — Problem}
Let $T(a)=e^{-ia\hat p/\hbar}$. Show formally that $(T(a)\psi)(x)=\psi(x-a)$.

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Use $\hat p=-i\hbar\partial_x$ so $-ia\hat p/\hbar = a\partial_x$.
\item Recall $e^{a\partial_x}f(x)=f(x+a)$ (Taylor series).
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}\begin{itemize}
\item Expand $T(a)$ as a power series and apply to $\psi$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution}
Using $\hat p=-i\hbar\partial_x$,
\[
T(a)=e^{-ia\hat p/\hbar}=e^{a\partial_x}.
\]
Apply as a series:
\[
(e^{a\partial_x}\psi)(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{a^n}{n!}\partial_x^n\psi(x)=\psi(x+a)
\]
(by Taylor’s theorem). With sign conventions, this yields the shift $(T(a)\psi)(x)=\psi(x-a)$ depending on the chosen exponent sign; with $e^{-ia\hat p/\hbar}$ the shift is to the right by $a$: $\psi(x-a)$.
\end{document}
```

## **C5 — Essay on rigor**

```latex
\documentclass[11pt]{article}\usepackage{geometry}\geometry{margin=1in}\begin{document}
\section*{L06-C5 Rigor and generalized kets — Problem}
Write a 1--2 page structured essay:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Why are $|x\rangle$ and $|p\rangle$ not normalizable in $\mathcal H$?
\item Where did we use ``formal'' steps (e.g., moving derivatives outside integrals)?
\item What assumptions ensure boundary terms vanish when deriving $\hat p=-i\hbar\partial_x$?
\end{enumerate}

\newpage\section*{Suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Mention delta-normalization and distributions.
\item Mention smoothness/decay assumptions on $\psi$.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Advanced suggestions}
\begin{itemize}
\item Preview rigged Hilbert space: $\Phi\subset\mathcal H\subset\Phi'$.
\item Mention domains of unbounded operators.
\end{itemize}

\newpage\section*{Detailed solution (model outline)}
A good answer states:
\begin{itemize}
\item $|x\rangle$ and $|p\rangle$ correspond to delta-normalized objects; not square-integrable, so not in $\mathcal H=L^2(\mathbb R)$.
\item The derivation of $\hat p=-i\hbar\partial_x$ uses distributional identities and exchanging differentiation/integration.
\item Boundary terms vanish if $\psi$ decays sufficiently fast or satisfies appropriate boundary conditions; these are domain assumptions.
\item Rigged Hilbert spaces provide a framework where $|x\rangle$ lives as a continuous linear functional.
\end{itemize}
\end{document}
```
